Editorial

GIS Day With Working Math

Day 19 was GIS Day, so projections felt right. But I wanted the visualization to actually show the math, not just talk about it.

Shape Shifter does this—the morphing animation is the transformation process. You watch Equirectangular bend into Mollweide, and the graticule lines show how the projection math warps coordinates. It's not decoration. The animation is the concept.

Tissot's Lie Detector takes the opposite approach. Instead of showing transformation, it reveals distortion. Those red circles look perfect when you generate them as actual circles on the globe. Put them through Mercator, and they stretch into massive ellipses near the poles. The geometry doesn't lie.

The dual-concept strategy worked better than I expected. Instead of testing one concept across 8-12 platforms (the usual approach), I tested whether Gemini 3 could handle two very different challenges. Turns out it could—both working in 3 prompts, polished in ~8 more. Total time: 30 minutes.

This is where Gemini 3 in AI Studio earns its spot as a Tier 1 platform. After Day 18's discovery that it suddenly works well for maps, Day 19 confirmed it. Fast initial results, clean D3 implementation, smooth animations, and mobile-responsive by default. The iteration loop is quick—each prompt adds something useful.

Both apps needed mobile fixes after deployment (touch targets, collapsible panels), but Gemini 3 implemented those in 2-3 prompts each. The preview-to-production gap is still there (always verify in production), but when it works, it works well.

GIS Day deserved maps that actually show the technical side of cartography. These two do that—one through transformation, one through distortion. The math made visible.
